# Season Arc

## Purpose

Define how a full season of MixHouse escalates across episodes — narratively, musically, and economically — so that creative, production, and music teams plan for the same trajectory.

## Key Takeaways

- Season length proposal: 8 episodes + 1 finale special.
- Three structural movements: Cast In, Crack Open, Headline Up.
- The season's central question is "which producer's voice is becoming undeniable?" — escalation is musical, not just dramatic.
- Music releases compound: by mid-season, contestants are competing against their own prior tracks for streaming attention.
- The finale is a public-facing tentpole — a `Final Set` at a real festival with a real label release.

## Core Content

### Proposed Episode Map (Season 1)

| Ep | Title (working) | Movement | Music release | Stake escalation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Move In | Cast In | 1 track per contestant or duo (intro EP) | Establish residency, brief, judges. |
| 2 | First Drop | Cast In | Week 2 tracks | First `Drop Night`; first crowd reaction. |
| 3 | The Crate | Crack Open | Theme: collaboration | Forced collaboration brief. |
| 4 | Mentors Week | Crack Open | Theme: signature sound | Major mentor drop; identity-defining tracks. |
| 5 | The Numbers | Crack Open | First mid-season elimination by streaming | Streaming performance carries elimination weight publicly. |
| 6 | Outside | Headline Up | First off-site performance | Contestants perform at a partner club / festival side stage. |
| 7 | Trial Sets | Headline Up | Solo extended set | Each finalist builds a 20–30 min DJ set. |
| 8 | Final Set | Headline Up | Tentpole festival headline + album reveal | Public, real-world finale. |
| Special | The Year After | Franchise | Season soundtrack + alumni tour reveal | Sets up next season casting. |

### Movement 1 — Cast In (Episodes 1–2)

- Establish residency, characters, judges, format.
- Get the audience invested in 12–16 names.
- First real music releases hit DSPs.
- Lowest-friction creative briefs to find the cast's true voices.

### Movement 2 — Crack Open (Episodes 3–5)

- Forced collaborations expose creative chemistry.
- Mentor drops escalate from peer to A-list.
- Streaming numbers begin to publicly influence eliminations.
- Mid-season cull halves the cast.

### Movement 3 — Headline Up (Episodes 6–8)

- Performance focus increases; competition shifts from track-craft to set-craft.
- Off-site performances at real venues.
- Final three perform full sets at the headline event.
- Season soundtrack reveal as a real commercial product.

### Compounding Music Releases

| Episode | Tracks live on DSPs (cumulative) |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | 12–16 |
| 2 | 24–32 |
| 4 | 40+ |
| 8 | 60+ |

By the finale, the season has a real-world catalogue. The soundtrack is not a tie-in product — it's the catalogue.

### The Central Question

Every season asks: *whose voice as a producer is becoming undeniable by the end?* Each elimination, mentor drop, and `Drop Night` should feel like another answer to that single question.

### Franchise Hooks

The arc plants explicit franchise seeds:
- **Alumni** — eliminated contestants reappear in finale specials, next-season Mentor Drops, and the touring package.
- **Format extensions** — Episode 5's "streaming-only" elimination foreshadows a `MixHouse: Streams` short-form spin-off.
- **International** — episode 6's off-site performance can be filmed in a future-territory location, signalling the franchise's portability.

### What the Arc Avoids

- "Power couples" sub-plots that crowd out craft.
- Lopsided early-favourite dominance — composite index keeps the field alive.
- Pre-finale soundtrack leaks — release sequencing is part of the IP.
