# Objection Matrix

## Purpose

Catalogue the strongest objections we expect from every audience and codify the canonical response to each. Any contributor encountering a new objection in the wild should add it here rather than answering it ad hoc.

## Key Takeaways

- Objections are predictable; surprise responses are not. Pre-rehearsed answers win meetings.
- Each objection has one canonical response, owned by a specific KB file for proof.
- Some objections are valid concessions, not rebuttals — say so. Pretending nothing is hard signals naivety.

## Core Content

### Investor Objections

| # | Objection | Canonical response | Proof file |
| - | --- | --- | --- |
| I1 | "Reality formats have flat or declining economics." | Licence-fee-only formats do. MixHouse is engineered as a multi-format franchise: licence + music + live + sponsorship + format sales. The flywheel is the asset. | `05_business_model/franchise_flywheel.md` |
| I2 | "Electronic music is niche outside Europe." | Festival data, streaming consumption, and producer-as-celebrity case studies show that electronic is now a global top-tier genre, particularly under 35. | `03_market_research/electronic_music_market.md` |
| I3 | "Single point of failure: if the music releases flop, the show flops." | Music releases are upside, not gating. The format works on character + competition + cinematic execution alone. Music is the multiplier. | `02_show_bible/format_engine.md` |
| I4 | "DJs are not characters. They face their laptops." | We cast for character, not just craft. Episode anatomy puts character moments in the residency house, the studio, and live performance — laptops are 15% of the runtime. | `02_show_bible/episode_anatomy.md`, `02_show_bible/contestant_archetypes.md` |
| I5 | "Music rights are a nightmare. You'll get crushed in publishing splits." | The music release pipeline is designed around a clean rights structure agreed with contestants at casting. The legal scaffolding is upside, not downside. | `05_business_model/music_rights_strategy.md` |

### Broadcaster / Streamer Objections

| # | Objection | Canonical response | Proof file |
| - | --- | --- | --- |
| B1 | "We've tried DJ shows — they don't rate." | Prior attempts were game-show or competition-only. None combined cinematic documentary, character residency, and live release pipeline. | `04_competitive_landscape/mixhouse_white_space.md` |
| B2 | "Cinematography at festival/club scale is operationally hard." | Cinematic crews already operate at festival scale (Cercle, Boiler Room, Coachella livestreams). Production model leverages this know-how. | `06_production_strategy/production_model.md` |
| B3 | "Won't this fragment our slate against other music formats?" | The franchise model means MixHouse is the music tentpole for years, not a one-off bet against rivals. | `05_business_model/franchise_flywheel.md` |
| B4 | "Demo is too narrow." | The crossover demo (`Drive to Survive`, `Cheer`, `Last Dance` viewers) plus the scene-core audience is broader than singing-show buyers assume. Pilot research will quantify. | `03_market_research/reality_tv_landscape.md` |

### Valid Concessions (Where the Objection is Partly Right)

| Topic | What we concede |
| --- | --- |
| First-season unknowns | Pilot is the proof. We're not claiming product-market fit pre-pilot. |
| Music release performance | Underwriting assumptions for streaming performance are conservative and dependent on label-partner execution. |
| International rollout pace | Format sales typically lag flagship season by 18–24 months. We don't fake faster. |
