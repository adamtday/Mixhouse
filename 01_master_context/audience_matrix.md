# Audience Matrix

## Purpose

Define every distinct audience MixHouse must address, what each one wants, fears, doubts, and requires as proof, what emotional trigger flips them from interest to action, and which website surfaces are responsible for each. The matrix is the source of truth for site architecture, page briefs, sponsorship targeting, casting outreach, press strategy, and investor sequencing.

## Key Takeaways

- MixHouse serves nine distinct audiences. Mixing their messages dilutes all of them.
- Each audience has a unique combination of want / fear / objection / required proof / emotional trigger. The combination, not any single attribute, defines the audience.
- Talent and Press are audiences, not resources. They pull the show into the world if served correctly and ignore or burn it if served badly.
- The website's progressive-disclosure model is a direct projection of this matrix. Every page and component is owned by one or two audiences.
- The matrix is the canonical input for every page brief in `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/`. Do not edit page briefs without revisiting this file.

## Core Content

### The Nine Audiences (Summary Matrix)

| # | Audience | Single most important thing they must believe | Single action we want |
| - | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Investors | The franchise compounds across music, live, and brand revenue, not just licence fees. | Term sheet on the format company or pilot tranche. |
| 2 | Streamers | This is the next franchisable prestige unscripted tentpole for under-35 audiences. | Commission a pilot or full season order. |
| 3 | Broadcasters | This is a territory-portable format with credible local-judge adaptation and clear ad / sponsor inventory. | Acquire territory rights or commission a local version. |
| 4 | Advisors | The format treats the scene with rigour and credits everyone fairly. | Lend name, intros, and creative input. |
| 5 | Sponsors | This delivers culturally-credible access to 18–34 electronic audiences at scale. | Sign category-exclusive integration LOI. |
| 6 | Talent (producers / DJs) | This is the most credible TV exposure available, with a fair rights deal and real career outcomes. | Apply to cast or refer peers. |
| 7 | Fans (core scene + crossover) | This is a real, beautiful, scene-respecting show about a culture they care about. | Watch, share, pre-save, attend. |
| 8 | Press | This is a substantive cultural artefact, not a publicity push. | Cover honestly and on-record. |
| 9 | Strategic partners (festivals, labels, DSPs, venues, agencies) | A multi-year relationship here is high-leverage for our IP and theirs. | Sign MoU or partner agreement. |

The rest of this document expands each audience. Use the per-audience sections when writing copy, page briefs, or outreach for that audience. Use the summary matrix when prioritising work.

### 1. Investors

- **Sub-segments.** Format-equity investors. Slate financiers. Music-tech crossover funds. Family offices with media exposure. Strategic media investors (record labels' venture arms, broadcaster equity vehicles).
- **What they want.** Multi-line, multi-year IP that compounds across catalogue, live, and format sales. Demo-aligned exposure that legacy broadcaster equity cannot deliver. A team that can execute cinematic and music operations simultaneously.
- **What they fear.** Single-line format companies dependent on broadcaster licence fees. Talent-led IP with no franchise mechanics. Speculative numbers from operators with no production track record. A "music company" with no real music supervision.
- **Their objections.** "Reality formats have flat economics." "Electronic is niche outside Europe." "Music IP from a TV show is small change." "If this worked, someone would have built it." See full responses in `01_master_context/objection_matrix.md` (Investor rows).
- **Required proof.** Franchise flywheel mechanism with named assets. Use of funds at line-item level. Comparable format-sale and music-catalogue economics. Production team CVs. Pilot evidence (post-pilot). Sponsor LOI count.
- **Emotional trigger.** Being early to the next `Idol`-scale music franchise, in a category where no incumbent has built anything yet. Pattern-matching this against the early days of `The Voice` format-sale economics or `Love Island` international rollout.
- **Where we serve them on the site.** `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/business_model.md` (L3 Substance). `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/materials.md` (L4 Gated, NDA on access). Investor variant of the canonical positioning line.

### 2. Streamers

- **Sub-segments.** Commissioning execs at Netflix, Amazon Prime Video, Apple TV+, Max, Disney+ unscripted, Paramount+, regional streamers (BBC iPlayer, ITVX, Channel 4 Streaming, RTL+, France TV Slash).
- **What they want.** A prestige-tier unscripted tentpole that pulls under-35 subscribers, retains them across a season, and franchises into territory adaptations. Multi-season order rationale before signing.
- **What they fear.** Another singing-competition format dressed in new clothes. A music format the scene rejects (which kills the talent and the press cycle). Casting that depends on celebrities they don't control. Operational complexity that blows the production schedule.
- **Their objections.** "We've tried DJ shows; they don't rate." "Cinematography at festival scale is operationally hard." "Demo is too narrow." "Music rights will be a nightmare." See `01_master_context/objection_matrix.md` (Broadcaster / Streamer rows).
- **Required proof.** Pilot sizzle reel. Casting funnel evidence. Advisor list with named scene credibility. Music rights deal template. Cost-per-hour benchmarks against prestige unscripted. Territory adaptation roadmap.
- **Emotional trigger.** Being the streamer that owned the format that defined the next era of music TV. The internal credibility of being credited with the next `Rhythm + Flow` × `Drive to Survive` combination.
- **Where we serve them on the site.** `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/the_show.md` (format intelligibility). `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/business_model.md` (commissioning rationale). `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/materials.md` (gated full pack including cast, advisors, format bible).

### 3. Broadcasters

- **Sub-segments.** Commissioning at BBC, Channel 4, ITV, Sky, ProSieben, France TV, RAI, Globo, TBS Japan. International production / format-acquisition arms (ITV Studios, Banijay, Endemol Shine, Fremantle).
- **What they want.** A scripted-grade unscripted format with a legible weekly mechanic, clear ad-break / sponsor inventory, a credible local-judge adaptation path, and a defensible age-rating story.
- **What they fear.** Streamer-exclusive economics that price them out. Format-sale terms that route music and live revenue away from their territory. Scheduling friction (a show that depends on a foreign festival window).
- **Their objections.** "Won't this fragment our slate against rival music formats?" "Demo is too narrow." "Local audiences won't sub-genre-relate." "Music rights are messy across territories."
- **Required proof.** Format bible. Sample local adaptation walk-through (UK / Germany / Brazil). Music rights template that supports local label partners. Ad-break / inventory map. Demo data from streamer-tier comparable shows.
- **Emotional trigger.** Owning the local-language version of a format that becomes a multi-decade national fixture, the way `The Voice` did in 60+ territories or `Love Island` did across multiple Anglo markets.
- **Where we serve them on the site.** Same as Streamers, plus an emphasis on the territory adaptation block in `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/business_model.md`.

### 4. Advisors

- **Sub-segments.** Scene-credible producers and DJs (active artists). Label A&R (Defected, Drumcode, Anjuna, Ninja Tune, XL, Warp). Festival promoters (Tomorrowland, Awakenings, Dekmantel). Agency partners (WME, CAA, UTA music; UK boutique agencies). Electronic-music journalists and editors (Resident Advisor, Mixmag, DJ Mag).
- **What they want.** A platform that respects how the scene actually works. A defined and credible scope (judging, mentor drops, A&R consult, public credit). Clarity that their endorsement won't be misused.
- **What they fear.** Being publicly associated with a format the scene rejects. Being treated as a celebrity prop instead of an operator. Discovering after signing that the rights deal exploits contestants.
- **Their objections.** "I'll lend my name only if you respect the culture." "What's actually expected of me?" "I don't want to be a TV personality." See `01_master_context/objection_matrix.md` (Advisor rows).
- **Required proof.** Cultural authenticity document (`07_brand_world/cultural_authenticity.md`). Anti-patterns document (`07_brand_world/anti_patterns.md`). Music rights deal in plain language. Names of co-advisors. Scope agreement showing concrete contributions and fee or equity structure.
- **Emotional trigger.** Helping shape the format that becomes the legacy artefact of the scene era they care about. Being able to point to MixHouse and say "this represents us correctly."
- **Where we serve them on the site.** `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/advisors.md` (primary surface, post-confirmation). `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/the_world.md` (cultural posture). Direct outreach not the site for first conversations.

### 5. Sponsors

- **Sub-segments.** Audio tech (headphones, monitors, controllers, synths). Beverage (energy, RTD, premium spirits, low/no-alc). Fashion (streetwear, performance, capsule collaborators). Gaming (platform, hardware, audio partners). Telco (carrier-level distribution, 5G activations). Automotive (EV / luxury, transport package). Hospitality and travel (residency-adjacent).
- **What they want.** Category-exclusive integration in a format that scene audiences trust. A multi-year inventory commitment, not a one-off campaign. Live, social, and editorial extension beyond linear screen time.
- **What they fear.** Scene-audience rejection ("inauthenticity tax"). Banner-style placement that reads as a sellout. Saturation against rival activations at festivals. Editorial veto by the format owner.
- **Their objections.** "Scene audiences reject branded content." "Festival sponsorships are saturated." "Why this and not a festival presenting deal?" See `01_master_context/objection_matrix.md` (Sponsor rows).
- **Required proof.** Inventory map (`05_business_model/sponsorship_inventory.md`). Integration principles (real product, real use, scene veto). Audience demo data. Category-exclusivity terms. Existing partner names (post-confirmation).
- **Emotional trigger.** Being the named partner that defined a category in this franchise era. Owning a presence in the next-era music tentpole that competitors can't buy into.
- **Where we serve them on the site.** `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/business_model.md` (sponsorship-inventory section). `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/materials.md` (gated sponsor pack). Direct outreach drives the relationship; the site is the supporting surface.

### 6. Talent (Producers / DJs)

- **Sub-segments.** Emerging producers (1–3 years releasing, scene-vouched). Mid-tier touring DJs without label deals. Duos and live-PA acts. Scene-known producers between contracts. Sub-genre representatives (afro house, amapiano, UK garage, drum and bass, peak-time techno, hyperpop crossover).
- **What they want.** Real TV exposure without the reality-TV stigma. Fair rights on released music. Scene-credible judges and mentors they respect. A path to real career outcomes (label deal, festival slot, tour) that wouldn't be available otherwise.
- **What they fear.** Villain edits. Loss of credibility. Music-rights traps that strip long-tail royalties. Being miscategorised as a sub-genre stereotype. The show's tone reading "cringe" to the scene.
- **Their objections.** "Reality TV will torch my credibility." "You'll edit me into a villain." "I'll lose ownership of my music." "It'll be cheesy." See `01_master_context/objection_matrix.md` (Talent rows).
- **Required proof.** Plain-English rights summary (derived from `05_business_model/music_rights_strategy.md`). Cultural authenticity posture (`07_brand_world/cultural_authenticity.md`). Named judges and mentors. Anti-patterns enforced publicly. Past alumni testimony (post-season-one).
- **Emotional trigger.** Going from "you know my SoundCloud" to "you know my festival slot" inside one season. Being recognised by the scene that made them, not just by a TV audience.
- **Where we serve them on the site.** `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/the_show.md` (casting CTA at end of page, with rights summary above the form). `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/the_world.md` (cultural posture). Casting form is the conversion surface.

### 7. Fans (Core Scene + Crossover)

- **Sub-segments.**
  - **Core scene fans.** 18–34. Already follow festivals, labels, club nights. High intent. Trust readout is binary: either we read as scene-native or we don't.
  - **Crossover viewers.** 18–44. Watch prestige unscripted (`Drive to Survive`, `Cheer`, `Last Dance`). Will sample MixHouse if the cinematography promises and the music converts. Bring volume.
- **What they want.** Atmosphere. Real characters. Music they can actually stream and attend live. The feeling that they discovered something before everyone else did.
- **What they fear.** Mainstream-TV cringe portrayal of dance music. Predictable competition mechanics. Performative scene-tourism. Music supervision that uses library beds over the contestants' own work.
- **Their objections.** "Is this another cringe TV version of dance music?" "Are the contestants real DJs?" "Will the music be good?" See `01_master_context/objection_matrix.md` (Audience rows).
- **Required proof.** Trailer cinematography. The advisor list (legitimacy signal). The cast (real producers, not auditionees). The released music (the show's own catalogue). Festival partner reveal (the finale stake is real).
- **Emotional trigger.**
  - Core scene: "This is for us, made by people who get it."
  - Crossover: "This looks like the next `Drive to Survive`, and the music is actually good."
- **Where we serve them on the site.** `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/home.md` (atmosphere, L1). `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/the_show.md` (format substance, L2). `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/the_world.md` (brand world, L2). Social and short-form distribute, the site is the deeper context.

### 8. Press

- **Sub-segments.**
  - **Scene press.** Resident Advisor, Mixmag, DJ Mag, Pitchfork (electronic desk), The Fader, Crack, Truants. Earn or lose first.
  - **Trade press.** Variety, Hollywood Reporter, Bloomberg, Music Business Worldwide, Music Ally, Deadline.
  - **Mainstream culture press.** New York Times culture desk, Guardian, FT Weekend, Vanity Fair, Vogue.
  - **Tech / streaming press.** The Verge, Wired, FT Tech, TechCrunch (deal coverage).
- **What they want.** A story their audience hasn't heard yet. Substantive access (set visits, cast time, advisor interviews). Embargoed access to numbers and partner reveals before competitors get them.
- **What they fear.** Being used as PR amplification for vapour-ware. Being held to embargoes that crack mid-cycle. Discovering after running a piece that the project is not what it claimed to be.
- **Their objections.** "This sounds like a marketing line, not a show." "Why should our readers care?" "Who's actually attached?"
- **Required proof.** EPK in `11_outputs/investor_materials/` or its press equivalent. Cast and advisor names on the record. Pilot footage at the right embargo tier. A specific story angle calibrated to the outlet (scene press wants the cultural artefact angle; trade press wants the franchise economics angle; mainstream wants the human stories of the cast; tech press wants the music-IP and short-form economics angle).
- **Emotional trigger.**
  - Scene press: being the publication that called this correctly before mainstream did.
  - Trade press: being early on a new prestige unscripted IP with named partners.
  - Mainstream: a great human story landing at the moment electronic culture goes mainstream.
- **Where we serve them on the site.** The site is the standing reference, not the lead. Press kit lives behind a soft gate (`08_website_strategy/page_briefs/materials.md`). Direct outreach drives the first article; the site supports follow-ups.

### 9. Strategic Partners

- **Sub-segments.** Festivals (anchor `Final Set` host, ADE / WMC partner cycles). Labels (release partner for the catalogue, sync partner for the IP). DSPs (Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon Music, Deezer; pre-save, editorial playlist support). Venues (`Drop Night` and tour). Agencies (WME, CAA, UTA, Wasserman, Paradigm). Live promoters (Live Nation Electronic, AEG Presents, Insomniac, Mixmag Live).
- **What they want.** A multi-year relationship that compounds for both sides. Defined IP and brand-safety terms. Mutual access to talent, audience, and inventory.
- **What they fear.** A short-term marketing partnership dressed as strategic. A format that under-delivers and tarnishes their brand. Exclusivity terms that lock them out of competitor activations.
- **Their objections.** "Why us, why now?" "What is the multi-year shape of this?" "How do you protect our IP and brand inside the format?" "What is the off-ramp if the format underperforms?"
- **Required proof.** Multi-year roadmap. Brand-safety governance. Mutual deal economics. Named first-team operators (Format Co., Music Supervision, MixHouse Live). Existing partner names (post-confirmation).
- **Emotional trigger.** Being the named anchor of an IP that defines the next era of their category (the festival that hosted the first `Final Set`, the label that broke the first MixHouse alumnus globally, the DSP that owned the editorial relationship from season one).
- **Where we serve them on the site.** Site is supplementary. Direct conversation is the lead. `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/business_model.md` and `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/materials.md` provide the standing reference.

### Audience Sequencing (Who Comes First)

Phase order matters. Each audience unlocks the next.

1. **Advisors first.** They unlock Talent quality, Press willingness to cover, and Streamer credibility.
2. **Talent second.** A casting funnel with scene-credible applicants is the precondition for everything downstream.
3. **Streamers and Broadcasters next.** With Advisors and a casting funnel in hand, commissioning conversations become substantive.
4. **Sponsors fourth.** Three LOI categories pre-broadcaster sign-off is a leading indicator commissioners reward.
5. **Investors fifth.** Investors price MixHouse against the four signals above. The deck is sharper with them landed.
6. **Strategic Partners parallel.** Festival anchor, label partner, DSP relationship typically progress alongside Investor / Streamer conversations.
7. **Press last.** Earned coverage lands once a substantive pack of (1)–(6) is signable. Earning press earlier risks vapour-ware perception.
8. **Fans launch-aligned.** The fan motion starts when there is something real to convert against (trailer, cast announcement, pre-save).

This sequencing is the source for the BD plan in `06_production_strategy/production_model.md` and the funding-tranche order in `06_production_strategy/use_of_funds.md`.

## Objections Answered

| Objection | Response |
| --- | --- |
| "Nine audiences is too many to message." | Each audience has a dedicated surface (page, gated pack, or direct outreach lane). The matrix prevents bleed; the site architecture enforces it page by page. |
| "Why split Streamers from Broadcasters?" | Their commissioning logic, sale terms, and territory considerations differ enough that one-message-fits-all dilutes both. They share most of `business_model.md` but receive different gated packs. |
| "Should Press really be an audience?" | Yes. Press behaviour drives talent application volume, advisor confidence, and sponsor LOI conversion. They are not just downstream amplifiers. |
| "Strategic Partners overlap with Sponsors." | Distinct. Sponsors buy inventory inside the format. Strategic Partners build mutual IP relationships (festival, label, DSP) outside the inventory frame. The deal structures and asks differ materially. |
| "Sponsors will see this and feel commodified." | They won't. The matrix is internal. External copy is built per-audience and never references the matrix structure. |
| "Talent is a resource, not an audience." | Treating Talent as a resource is the standard reality-TV failure mode. The casting funnel collapses when scene-credible producers see themselves as resources rather than partners. |

## Website Implications

- Every page brief in `08_website_strategy/page_briefs/` must declare its primary and secondary audiences from this matrix.
- The progressive-disclosure model (`08_website_strategy/progressive_disclosure_model.md`) is the depth-ladder for this matrix. Any update here cascades there.
- Microcopy decisions (CTA labels, form fields, gating thresholds) follow per-audience emotional triggers documented above.
- The newsletter and pre-save flows belong to Fans; the Materials gate belongs to Investors / Streamers / Broadcasters / Sponsors / Press / Strategic Partners.

## Open Questions

- Should we further split Fans into "Core Scene" and "Crossover" as separate top-level audiences? Current answer: kept as one audience with sub-segments because the website's L1 / L2 surfaces serve both simultaneously. Revisit if conversion data after launch shows different content needs.
- Sizing each audience: minimum conversion volume needed per audience before greenlight. Owner: `05_business_model/investor_case.md`.
- Should there be a separate "Existing Industry Operators" audience (broadcaster acquisitions / format-sale arms) distinct from Streamers and Broadcasters? Possibly. Hold until format-sale strategy is concrete in `05_business_model/franchise_flywheel.md`.
- Do we need region-specific audience matrices for territory adaptation, or does this matrix translate? Hypothesis: same matrix, with sub-segment swap-outs per territory.
