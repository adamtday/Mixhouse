# Rhythm + Flow

## Purpose

Identify what Netflix's `Rhythm + Flow` proved about streamer-scale music competition formats, what its limits were, and what MixHouse should learn vs do differently.

## Key Takeaways

- `Rhythm + Flow` proved that streamers can commission and rate a music competition at prestige tier when paired with credible judges.
- It is the closest streamer comparable to MixHouse, same buyer category, same prestige tier, music-led.
- It is hip-hop-only, audition-led, and shy of true franchise economics across multiple seasons.
- MixHouse is its genre-and-format-engine successor, not a duplicate.

## Core Content

### What It Proves

- A streamer (Netflix) will commission a music-led competition at scripted-adjacent cinematography.
- A panel of genuinely credible scene judges (Cardi B, Chance the Rapper, T.I.) unlocks both talent supply and audience trust.
- Single-genre depth beats multi-genre breadth.
- Real-world progression (city auditions → finale) makes the world legible.

### What MixHouse Adopts

- Streamer-first commissioning posture and prestige aesthetic budget.
- Scene-credible judges as the format's reputational anchor.
- Single-genre depth (electronic) as a feature.

### What MixHouse Improves On

- Replaces audition-and-cypher with residency-and-creation, a continuous creative engine, not episodic auditions.
- Adds real music releases per episode, not just freestyles.
- Adds a recurring `Drop Night` and `Final Set` payoff.
- Designs explicitly for territory adaptation; Netflix-only is a strategic ceiling.

### Where It Plateaued

- Multi-season cadence not yet established as a reliable annual franchise.
- Real post-show economics (releases, tours) modest relative to format potential.
- Limited live-event extensions.

### Lessons for the Slot Shape

- Streamers are buying music-led prestige unscripted, the slot is real.
- They are not buying generic singing competitions, they are buying scene credibility + cinematic execution.
