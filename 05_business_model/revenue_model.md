# Revenue Model

## Purpose

State, at the level of named line items and the order of magnitude of each, the revenue stack MixHouse is designed to capture across its lifecycle, and the dependencies between those lines.

## Key Takeaways

- MixHouse has five primary revenue lines: production fee + premium, music IP, live events, sponsorship/brand, format sales.
- The first three are seasonally recurring; the last two compound over time.
- Music IP is the line that turns each season into a multi-year asset rather than a one-year licence.
- The model is intentionally diversified to remove single-line dependency risk that crushes most format companies.
- All dollar/percentage figures here are placeholders pending pilot economics.

## Core Content

### The Five Revenue Lines

| Line | Description | Cadence | Owner file |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Production Fee + Premium** | Cost-plus production fee from broadcaster/streamer, plus a back-end premium tied to performance. | Per-season | `06_production_strategy/production_model.md` |
| **Music IP (publishing + masters)** | Royalty share from in-show releases across publishing and master ownership. | Multi-year tail | `05_business_model/music_rights_strategy.md` |
| **Live Events** | `Final Set`, finalists' tour, alumni live activations. | Annual | `05_business_model/live_event_extensions.md` |
| **Sponsorship / Brand Partnerships** | Category-exclusive sponsor inventory across show, live, and platform. | Multi-year | `05_business_model/sponsorship_inventory.md` |
| **Format Sales / Territory Adaptation** | Licensing the format to local broadcasters/producers internationally. | Multi-year compound | `05_business_model/franchise_flywheel.md` |

### Dependencies Between Lines

- Music IP depends on the release pipeline being operational from S1.
- Live events depend on a credible festival anchor for the `Final Set`: booked before the season airs.
- Sponsorship depends on the demo profile being validated, which depends on the pilot landing.
- Format sales depend on the flagship season delivering a numeric benchmark that local territories can underwrite.

These dependencies are the most important risk-management items in `06_production_strategy/production_risks.md`.

### Cost Structure (high-level)

The cost picture is owned by `06_production_strategy/production_model.md`. Headline lines:

- Production crew + cinematic capture
- Talent compensation and music rights advances
- Residency location and operations
- Music release pipeline and label partner costs
- Live event production (`Drop Night`, `Final Set`)
- Marketing and short-form distribution
- Corporate / format-company overhead

### Pricing Posture

- Production fees benchmark to **prestige unscripted CPHs**, not legacy reality.
- Music IP deals follow **industry-standard splits with producers** (transparent and competitive).
- Sponsorship inventory **priced at festival-sponsorship benchmarks**, not generic TV ad rates.
- Format sales priced **per territory with a floor and ratchet**.

### Why a Diversified Stack

- One-line revenue (production fee only) is the standard format-company death trap.
- A diversified stack with multi-year compounding lines is closer to a music-publishing company's economics, significantly more valuable per dollar of revenue.

## Objections Answered

| Objection | Response |
| --- | --- |
| "Music IP from a TV show is small change." | Sixty tracks per season released through a credible label structure, accruing over years across territories, this is closer to a publishing catalogue than a soundtrack album. |
| "Format sales lag the flagship by years." | Yes, and the model assumes that. The flywheel compounds across years 3–5, not from year 1. |
| "Sponsorship saturation in music is real." | We sell category exclusivity and integration, not banner ads. The unit price is higher and the inventory smaller. |
