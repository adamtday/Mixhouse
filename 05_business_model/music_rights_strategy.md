# Music Rights Strategy

## Purpose

Set the rights structure governing the music created on MixHouse, who owns what, how royalties flow, what's released through whom, so that the format is commercially defensible and talent feels fairly treated.

## Key Takeaways

- Rights structure is transparent, pre-disclosed at casting, and competitive with mainstream-label deals.
- MixHouse (the format company) co-owns a defined share of masters and a share of publishing; producers retain credit and meaningful long-tail royalty.
- A dedicated label partner (or in-house imprint) handles releases.
- Sync and territory licensing roll up to the format company; touring rights stay with contestants.
- Anti-pattern: any structure that traps contestants or feels exploitative ends the brand.

## Core Content

### Rights Layer Map

| Layer | Owned by | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Composition / Publishing** | Shared between contestant(s), co-writers, and MixHouse publishing entity. | Contestant always retains substantive share. |
| **Master Recording** | Co-owned: MixHouse format company / label partner + contestant(s). | Share defined per contract, with industry-credible split. |
| **Sound Recording (Drop Night live capture)** | MixHouse format company. | Captured at venue under standard performance-rights framework. |
| **On-Air / Format Use** | MixHouse for the format and any spin-offs / format adaptations. | Standard sync-and-format-use rider. |
| **Sync (post-show)** | Routed through MixHouse / label partner. | Producers share net sync revenue. |
| **Touring Performance Rights** | Contestants retain. | MixHouse takes a defined cut on tours promoted by MixHouse Live. |

### Headline Principles

1. **Transparent at casting.** Contract terms are summarised in plain English and shown to applicants pre-application.
2. **Producers credited everywhere.** Songwriting and producer credits are mandatory across all releases.
3. **No buyouts.** No structure that strips long-tail royalties from contestants.
4. **No exclusivity beyond the season + a defined option window.** Contestants are not locked to MixHouse careers indefinitely.
5. **Mentor / co-writer shares are documented in real time.** No retroactive credit disputes.

### Label Strategy Options

| Option | Pros | Cons |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Major label imprint partnership** | Distribution muscle, editorial DSP access, brand validation. | Slower deal cycles, harder economics on splits. |
| **Independent label partnership** | Faster cycles, better splits, scene credibility. | Less editorial DSP support. |
| **In-house label** | Maximum control, full margin. | Operational complexity; takes years to mature. |

Recommended posture: **independent label partnership for S1**, with strategic relationships at majors for sync, plus an in-house imprint option from S3 once scale justifies.

### Release Pipeline Integration

The legal scaffolding lives here; the operational pipeline lives in `06_production_strategy/music_release_pipeline.md`. The two must stay synced:

- Casting → rights briefing pack signed by applicants.
- Pre-production → producer-by-producer contracts confirming splits, credits, and label assignment.
- Production → real-time logging of contributions (mentors, co-writers, vocalists).
- Release → metadata aligned with contract terms.
- Post-release → royalty statements quarterly.

### Anti-Patterns

- "Universal rights for promotional use" without bounded definition.
- Recoupable advances that wipe out modest streaming earnings.
- Mandatory exclusivity on follow-up releases.
- Mentor credit-grabbing.
- Opaque sync deals.

### Cultural Authenticity Tie-In

Music rights are a load-bearing piece of cultural authenticity. A bad rights deal would be visible to the scene within months and would torpedo casting in S2+. See `07_brand_world/cultural_authenticity.md`.

## Objections Answered

| Objection | Response |
| --- | --- |
| "If contestants own less, the format makes more money." | True short-term, false long-term. Rights deals that look exploitative kill the casting funnel within two seasons. |
| "Why not do straight buyouts?" | Buyouts are cheap *now* and very expensive once the brand has matured. We deliberately leave residual value with contestants. |
