# Production Model

## Purpose

State how MixHouse is produced, who owns the format, who produces each season, where decisions live, and what scaffolding is reusable across seasons and territories.

## Key Takeaways

- Format ownership lives with a dedicated MixHouse format company. Production is delivered via a partner production company or in-house production unit, depending on territory.
- The format company keeps IP, music rights, sponsorship, and franchise economics. The production partner delivers the show.
- A small, persistent format-team controls cultural, creative, and brand decisions across territories.
- Music release pipeline and platform are operated centrally (not by production partners) to preserve catalogue and audience value.

## Core Content

### Entity Structure

| Entity | Owns | Operates |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **MixHouse Format Co.** | Format IP, brand, music rights, platform, sponsorship inventory, advisor network. | Format standards, brand world, music supervision, advisor governance. |
| **Production Partner (per territory)** | Production deliverables. | Season delivery (crew, location, post-production). |
| **MixHouse Live (subsidiary or JV)** | Live event IP and tour. | `Final Set`, tour, alumni events. |
| **MixHouse Records (in-house or partner imprint)** | Master ownership (shared with contestants per `05_business_model/music_rights_strategy.md`). | Releases, DSP relationships. |

### Roles and Ownership

| Role | Who owns it | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Format Bible | Format Co. | Source of truth across territories. |
| Casting | Format Co. + Production Partner | Quality and brand discipline. |
| Music Supervision | Format Co. (centralised) | Catalogue value depends on consistent control. |
| Sponsorship | Format Co. | Multi-territory anchor partners; consistency. |
| Production delivery | Production Partner | Local crew, location, infrastructure. |
| Live events | MixHouse Live | Year-round operating cadence. |

### Territory Adaptation Model

Each territory replicates a known pattern:

1. Format Co. licenses the bible to a local production partner.
2. Local Music Supervisor reports to central Music Supervision.
3. Local judges and mentors approved by Format Co.
4. Local sponsorship inventory negotiated within the central category framework.
5. Local live events handled by MixHouse Live with regional promoter partners.

### Why Not Produce In-House Globally

- Production at cinematic scale in multiple territories simultaneously is operationally hostile.
- Local production partners bring crew relationships, tax incentives, and union know-how.
- Format Co. captures the franchise margins; production partner captures delivery margin. Both incentives align.

### Why Keep Music + Platform In-House

- These are the *compounding* lines. Outsourcing them gives away the franchise's most valuable assets.
- Central control prevents catalogue fragmentation across territories.

### Advisor Governance

- Named advisors are listed publicly (post-confirmation) on the website.
- Advisor scope defined explicitly per individual: judging, talent intros, A&R consult, public credit.
- Advisor equity / fees structured per contribution tier.

### Decision Rights Map

| Decision | Made by |
| --- | --- |
| Cast (final ensemble) | Format Co. with Production Partner input. |
| Judges | Format Co. |
| Mentor Drops | Format Co. + Music Supervision. |
| Music releases | Music Supervision + Label Partner. |
| Sponsorship inventory and brand fit | Format Co. |
| Edit (final cut) | Production Partner with Format Co. approval. |
| Live event programming | MixHouse Live with Format Co. approval. |

### Anti-Patterns

- A format company that has lost music rights to a label partner.
- A territory adaptation that diverges from the bible enough to break franchise consistency.
- A production partner with veto power over casting or judges.

## Objections Answered

| Objection | Response |
| --- | --- |
| "Splitting format and production adds overhead." | The opposite, it eliminates the overhead of trying to be a cinematic production company in every territory. |
| "Music supervision in-house is expensive." | It's a margin-protective expense. Outsourcing music ends the franchise at lower value. |
