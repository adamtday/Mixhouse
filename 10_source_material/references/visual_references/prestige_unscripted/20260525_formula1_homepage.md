# Formula 1 Homepage

## Observed Page Content

Hero leads with the latest race result: full-width hero image, headline "Antonelli wins dramatic Canadian GP as Russell retires". This positions race outcomes as the primary narrative driver.

Architecture follows a hierarchical cascade:
- Primary zone: race result hero + related news cards
- Secondary zones: schedule carousel (previous / next races), standings tables, featured video gallery
- Tertiary navigation: Drivers, Teams, News, Fantasy gaming grouped into consistent dropdown menus
- Footer: partner logos, social links, app download prompts

Copy adopts a direct, urgent register. Headlines favour punchy, colloquial phrasing capturing driver quotes verbatim:
- "We can do better"
- "put us out of our misery"
- "not really the way I wanted"

Body copy remains functional and result-focused rather than narrative or reflective.

Trackside photography dominates. Video content is prominently featured (eight race highlight clips observed). Images emphasise emotional moments: celebrations, retirements, on-track action. Consistent colour grading and aspect ratios (16:9 predominant).

Content pillars:
- Races (schedule with carousel navigation, past results)
- Drivers and Teams (full roster links with standardised profile imagery)
- News (tagged articles with thumbnail cards)
- Fantasy / Gaming (F1 TV, F1 Manager, F1 Clash segregated as dedicated category)
- Commerce (Authentics, Store, Tickets, Hospitality in top navigation)

Standings table prioritises visual hierarchy. Partner logos occupy substantial real estate (LVMH, Pirelli, Aramco, AWS visible). Engagement survey CTA interrupts editorial flow. Mixed-series content (F1, F2, F1 Academy).

## Mechanism Analysis

F1.com is the most strategically relevant reference for MixHouse's "show plus sport plus brand world" hybrid:

1. **Result-as-hero on every visit.** The home page is reset by the latest race result. Audiences who return between events see different content; the page is alive.
2. **Driver-quote headlines as voice.** Verbatim driver quotes do the headline work. The page's editorial voice is the athletes' voice, not the marketing team's voice.
3. **Schedule-as-living-spine.** Previous and next race appear in a carousel; the sport's rhythm is the page's rhythm.
4. **Commerce surfaces compartmented in top navigation.** Tickets, hospitality, and commerce are accessible but do not interrupt the editorial flow.

## What Should Translate To MixHouse

- Result-as-hero pattern for MixHouse home page during active cycles: the latest cycle moment becomes the home-page lead, refreshing on each cycle.
- Verbatim-quote-headlines from producers, mentors, judges as a voice discipline. Editorial voice is the cast's voice, not the marketing team's voice.
- Schedule-as-living-spine for the cycles-and-finale calendar. The next cycle and finale anchor the page rhythm.
- Commerce compartmentalisation: any MixHouse commerce surfaces (merchandise, finale tickets, partnership inquiries) live in top navigation, not in editorial flow.

## What Should NOT Translate To MixHouse

- The dense partner-logo footer pattern (MixHouse footer is editorial-restrained; named partnerships appear in editorial context, not as logo wall).
- The engagement-survey-modal interruption pattern (MixHouse refuses engagement-bait interruption per `anti-overstimulated-UI` principle).
- The Fantasy / Gaming segregated commerce category (not relevant to MixHouse).
- The multi-series content mixing (F1, F2, F1 Academy); MixHouse is single-franchise.
