# Resident Advisor Homepage

## Observed Page Content

Top-level navigation separates five content pillars: Events, Music, Magazine, a "2000-25" feature link, and account access. Regional view (Minneapolis / St Paul observed at capture) demonstrates location-specific gatekeeping. Secondary menu organises deeper content: Festivals, RA Podcast, Reviews, Mix of the Day, Playlists.

Modular card-based home layout. "Latest News" leads with time-stamped editorial entries, followed by "Popular News" ranked by engagement. Below: Minneapolis event listings organised by "Popular events" and "RA Picks" (curated selections). Magazine content appears as feature cards with publication dates, bylines, and descriptive copy.

Content categories observed:
- Events: dated listings with venue links, attendance counts, brief editorial context.
- News: timestamped announcements mixing festival lineups, artist deaths, scene analysis, music releases.
- Music: album / single reviews, podcast episodes, curated playlists.
- Magazine: long-form features grouped by series (Art of DJing, Best Music, Festivals).

Editorial tone balances insider vernacular with accessibility. Scene-specific language ("footwork sound", "baile funk", "IDM icons") used without explanation; readers are assumed to understand. Sample editorial lines:
- "Rhythmic mastery, live from Ormside Projects"
- "A head-scrambling mix from the Tokyo wax specialist"
- "Chicago's beloved king of Queen! Derrick Carter heads up to Minneapolis..."

Image placement: consistent rectangular cards with artist / event photography. Footer: regional selector, language toggle, legal links, social media across eight platforms.

## Mechanism Analysis

RA's homepage is a scene-credibility masterclass:

1. **Assumed-knowledge editorial voice.** The publication does not explain scene vocabulary. This is the strongest possible signal that the publication is by and for the scene. Outsiders are welcome but not catered to.
2. **Location-as-first-class entity.** The regional selector and city-specific event listings make the city the primary editorial unit. Scene happens in places; RA respects that.
3. **News, events, magazine, music, reviews as parallel pillars.** No single content type dominates. The publication holds together because each pillar serves a specific scene need; the home page lets readers self-route.

## What Should Translate To MixHouse

- Assumed-knowledge editorial voice for scene-facing surfaces and for music-led copy throughout the site. Scene vocabulary is not explained; outsiders learn by reading.
- Location-as-first-class entity for the residency-and-finale geography. The cities MixHouse operates in are first-class editorial subjects, not background.
- Parallel-pillar information architecture for the broader site: show, world, audience, business model exist as parallel surfaces, not as a single funnel.

## What Should NOT Translate To MixHouse

- The high-density home-page card-grid pattern (MixHouse home page leads with single-image-per-frame restraint, not modular density).
- The events-listing-as-primary pattern (MixHouse is not a ticketing platform).
- The eight-platform social-footer pattern (MixHouse footer is editorial-restrained; social presence is curated and named).
- The location-gated regional selector pattern (MixHouse is single-region per cycle, not multi-region simultaneously).
